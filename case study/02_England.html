1. Abstract

England deployed a large-scale centralised contact tracing programme, NHS Test and Trace, on 28 May 2020. The programme initially relied entirely on manual telephone-based tracing. The NHS COVID-19 app, a decentralised Bluetooth-based digital tool, was added on 24 September 2020, creating a hybrid system that operated until routine contact tracing ended on 24 February 2022.

Once a case tested PCR-positive, their result was automatically registered in the NHS Test and Trace system, which then contacted the case to obtain details of close contacts. Separately, the case could manually enter their positive result into the app to trigger automatic anonymous notifications to recent Bluetooth contacts.

Contacts identified through manual tracing received a legal 10-day self-isolation order, while contacts notified only by the app received an advisory recommendation, with no legal enforcement.

2. Background

2.1 Epidemic context

England confirmed its first two COVID-19 cases on 31 January 2020 in York. The epidemic progressed through four broad phases, characterised by successive dominant variants and changes in public health response.

PhasePeriodDominant variantEpidemiological characteristic
1Jan–Nov 2020Wild-typeFirst wave; first national lockdown in March 2020; NHS Test and Trace launched in May 2020.
2Dec 2020–May 2021AlphaPositivity peaked in January 2021 across all regions; NHS app operational.
3May–Dec 2021DeltaPositivity remained elevated from July to December 2021; vaccination rollout accelerated.
4Dec 2021–Feb 2022OmicronHighest recorded prevalence; routine contact tracing formally ended on 24 February 2022.

2.2 Enabling conditions

Two structural factors shaped England's approach. First, Public Health England's existing tracing capacity was designed for small outbreaks rather than community-scale transmission, prompting the creation of a new centralised national service with heavy reliance on private contractors and rapid recruitment of approximately 25,000 contact tracers. Second, an initial attempt to develop a centralised Bluetooth app was abandoned in June 2020 in favour of the decentralised Google/Apple Exposure Notification framework, delaying app launch by four months and shaping its legal and data architecture.

3. Contact Tracing Strategy

3.1 System architecture

From September 2020, England operated two parallel and independent contact tracing pathways: manual tracing through NHS Test and Trace and digital tracing through the NHS COVID-19 app. The two pathways did not share data or coordinate notifications, and they carried different legal weight. A single confirmed case could trigger both pathways simultaneously.

Manual pathway: NHS Test and Trace

  1. Case tests PCR-positive; result automatically enters NHS Test and Trace.
  2. NHS Test and Trace proactively contacts the case by phone or text.
  3. Case verbally recalls known contacts during a phone interview.
  4. NHS Test and Trace contacts each named contact individually.
  5. Contact receives a legal self-isolation order.

Digital pathway: NHS COVID-19 app

  1. Case tests PCR-positive and manually enters the positive result or test reference code in the app.
  2. App calculates which Bluetooth contacts met the risk threshold.
  3. App sends anonymous exposure notifications to qualifying contacts.
  4. Contact receives notification; case and contact identities remain unknown to each other.
  5. Contact receives advisory recommendation to self-isolate, with no legal obligation.

3.3 Implementation phases

Phase 1 — Manual only (28 May 2020–23 September 2020)

NHS Test and Trace launched on 28 May 2020 with telephone-based tracing only. Approximately 25,000 contact tracers were recruited. In the first week, 85% of identified contacts were reached and advised to self-isolate. The initial NHSX Bluetooth app was abandoned in June 2020 after technical concerns, and development switched to the decentralised Google/Apple framework.

Phase 2 — Hybrid: manual + digital app (24 September 2020–23 February 2022)

The NHS COVID-19 app launched on 24 September 2020 in England and Wales. It was regularly used by approximately 16.5 million users, around 28% of the population, by the end of 2020. The app operated independently of manual tracing: cases self-reported positive results and the app automatically notified recent Bluetooth contacts anonymously.

Phase 3 — End of programme (24 February 2022)

Routine contact tracing formally ended on 24 February 2022 as England adopted a “living with COVID” strategy. Free testing ended on 1 April 2022.

4. Notification & Quarantine Mechanism

4.1 Cases: PCR-positive individuals

DimensionEngland policy
TriggerSymptom onset or positive PCR/LFT result, whichever occurred first.
Isolation locationHome only; no central government quarantine facility option.
Duration10 days from symptom onset, or positive test date if asymptomatic.
Legal statusLegally enforced; breach was a criminal offence, with fines starting at £1,000.

4.2 Contacts — manual tracing notification

Manual tracing was exposure-triggered. Contacts were notified and required to isolate immediately upon notification, not upon symptom onset. Isolation was home-based, with no central facility option. Duration evolved from 14 days to 10 days, then to exemption for fully vaccinated contacts, before all legal requirements ended on 24 February 2022.

4.3 Contacts — NHS COVID-19 app notification

App notification was exposure-triggered and occurred automatically within approximately 4 hours after a positive test was entered. Contacts notified solely by the app were advised to self-isolate, but they were not subject to a legal duty to self-isolate.

5. Key Data & Evidence

5.1 Coverage / contacts reached

The main coverage metric for England is the proportion of identified close contacts successfully reached by NHS Test and Trace. This is the most direct real-world equivalent of a tracing coverage parameter for manual tracing.

PeriodContacts reachedReference
28 May–3 June 202085% in first week; 90.5% recorded in early DHSC bulletin contextDHSC Weekly Bulletin, Week 1
4 June–10 June 202090.6%DHSC Weekly Bulletin, Week 2
4 February–10 February 202186.2% cumulative contact reach; 74.3% reached within 24 hoursDHSC weekly statistics
Critical quality issue: cases managed by local Health Protection Teams had a median of 25 contacts identified per case, while cases managed by the centralised national call centre had a median of only 2 contacts per case — a 12-fold difference.

5.2 Tracing delays

  • SAGE recommended no more than 48 hours between case identification and contacts self-isolating to break transmission chains.
  • 74.3% of cases were reached within 24 hours of transfer to the NHS Test and Trace system in February 2021, but this clock starts after transfer, not from test result or symptom onset.
  • The NHS COVID-19 app notified contacts within approximately 4 hours of a positive test being entered, substantially faster than the manual pathway.

5.4 Rt / epidemic-level impact

Not available as a clean independent estimate. NHS Test and Trace activity was deployed alongside concurrent NPIs. The cited reduction in R was contested, and the contact-tracing-specific contribution should not be used as a direct model input without sensitivity analysis.

6. Limitations & Failure Points

  • Low self-isolation compliance among contacts: parliamentary surveys found full compliance ranging from 10% to 59%, while ONS surveys reported higher self-reported compliance of 86%–93%. The gap reflects measurement method differences.
  • Contact depth gap: local Health Protection Teams identified a median of 25 contacts per case, compared with 2 for the national call centre.
  • App notifications had no legal enforcement: contacts notified solely by the app had no legal duty to self-isolate, limiting behavioural impact compared with manually traced contacts.
  • Delta wave disruption: during summer 2021, mass app notifications caused widespread economic disruption, contributing to the August 2021 decision to exempt vaccinated contacts and reducing the system's effective reach.
  • Budget and value-for-money concerns: NHS Test and Trace spent £13.5 billion in 2020/21 and £16 billion in 2021/22; the National Audit Office found insufficient evidence of value for money.
